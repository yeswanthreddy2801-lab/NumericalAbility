DATA INTERPRETATION — TABLES (DETAILED NOTES)

============================================================
1. PURPOSE & APPROACH

Tables present structured data (rows × columns) and are ideal for compactly representing multi-dimensional information (e.g., product vs month sales). Approach table problems by scanning headers, units, and totals first; then extract only the cells needed for the question.

============================================================
2. TABLE STRUCTURE & TERMINOLOGY

- Row: horizontal record (e.g., product A).
- Column: vertical field (e.g., Jan sales).
- Cell: intersection containing the data value.
- Header: identifies rows/columns; always read it.
- Subtotals/totals: rows or columns that sum partial or full data.

============================================================
3. COMMON QUESTION TYPES

- Totals and subtotals (row-wise, column-wise).
- Percentages and shares (part/whole).
- Averages (mean of selected cells).
- Ratios between rows/columns.
- Growth or change (absolute or percent) across periods.
- Conditional queries (e.g., count rows where value > X).

============================================================
4. ESSENTIAL FORMULAS & PATTERNS

- Sum(row) = Σ cells in the row.
- Sum(column) = Σ cells in the column.
- Percentage of total = (cell / grand total) × 100.
- Average of k values = (Σ selected values) / k.
- Ratio A:B = (sum of A-row / sum of B-row) or use cell-wise ratio as asked.
- Percent change = (new − old)/old × 100%.

Pro tip: Precompute row totals and column totals if multiple questions reference the same table.

============================================================
5. STEP-BY-STEP PROBLEM-SOLVING WORKFLOW

1) Read title, headers and units.
2) Identify which rows/columns are relevant.
3) Note any given totals or subtotals.
4) Perform minimal arithmetic: prefer subtraction/addition over recalculating entire totals.
5) Use ratios and percentages to avoid computing absolute totals when possible.
6) Validate by checking that computed row/column totals sum to grand total.

============================================================
6. SHORTCUTS & OPTIMIZATIONS

- Use differences: If two columns sum to a known total, the third = total − (other two).
- Work with percentages or fractions if totals are large or unknown: assume convenient total (100 or LCM) when necessary.
- For average-of-subset, compute (grand total − excluded sum)/(remaining count).
- When multiple queries use same sums, cache intermediate results (rowTotals, colTotals, grandTotal).

============================================================
7. WORKED EXAMPLES

Example 1 — Sales table
Table: Products A,B,C across Jan–Mar
- A: Jan=120, Feb=150, Mar=130
- B: Jan=100, Feb=120, Mar=110
- C: Jan=80,  Feb=90,  Mar=100

Q1: Total sales of A across three months?
- Sum = 120+150+130 = 400.

Q2: Percentage share of B in March if grand total (all products, all months) = 1200?
- B_Mar = 110 → share = 110/1200 ×100 = 9.166...%.

Q3: Average monthly sale of C?
- Avg = (80+90+100)/3 = 90.

Example 2 — Missing value
If column totals are Jan=300, Feb=360, Mar=340 and A,B values known except A_Mar missing: A_Mar = MarTotal − (B_Mar + C_Mar).

============================================================
8. TABLE COMPARISONS & DERIVED METRICS

- Growth rate per product: (MarTotal − JanTotal)/JanTotal ×100.
- Contribution to monthly total: rowCell / columnTotal ×100.
- Weighted average: when combining rows with different weights use sum(value×weight)/sum(weights).

============================================================
9. PRACTICE PROBLEMS (ANSWERS FOLLOW)

Given table: Monthly visitors (in hundreds)

	Jan  Feb  Mar
Site A  120  150  130
Site B  100  120  110
Site C   80   90  100

1) Grand total visitors for the quarter?  Ans: 120+150+130 +100+120+110 +80+90+100 = 1000 (hundreds) → 100,000.
2) Which site had highest average monthly visitors?  Ans: A with average 400/3 ≈ 133.33 (hundreds).
3) Percentage of visitors for Site B in Feb (of month total)?  Ans: B_Feb=120; month total Feb=150+120+90=360 → 120/360×100 = 33.33%.
4) If Site C increases Mar by 20%, what is new grand total?  Ans: C_Mar becomes 120 → new grand total = 1000 + 20 (hundreds) = 1020 (hundreds).
5) Ratio of Jan totals of A:B:C?  Ans: 120:100:80 = 12:10:8 or simplified 6:5:4.

============================================================
10. MULTI-STEP & CONDITIONAL QUERIES

- For "how many months product exceeded X" questions, scan column-wise and count cells > X.
- For conditional averages (e.g., average of products with sales > Y), sum qualifying cells and divide by count of qualifying months.
- For percentage increase asked when a value is removed or added, recompute grand total first before percentage calculation.

============================================================
11. COMMON MISTAKES & VERIFICATION

- Mistaking row vs column (transpose error): always read headers.
- Incorrect unit conversion (thousands vs units): standardize units first.
- Forgetting to convert to percentages when asked share of total.

Verification steps:
1) Check row totals and column totals consistency.
2) Ensure percentages sum to ~100% when grouped correctly.
3) Recompute a single value by an alternate method to validate.

============================================================
12. TIPS FOR SPEED

- Scan for totals/subtotals first — many questions use them.
- Use elimination on MCQs by quick approximate calculations.
- Memorize common fraction-percent conversions (1/3≈33.33%, 1/8=12.5%, etc.).
