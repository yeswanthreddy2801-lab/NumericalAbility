PIPES AND CISTERNS - DETAILED NOTES

1. INTRODUCTION
Pipes and cisterns problems are a part of the Time and Work topic in aptitude exams and competitive tests. These problems involve filling and emptying a tank using inlet pipes, outlet pipes, or leaks. The key idea is to compute the net work done per hour instead of the total work.

2. FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS
- Tank capacity is treated as 1 unit.
- Inlet pipe fills the tank; its rate is positive.
- Outlet pipe empties the tank; its rate is negative.
- Leak behaves like an outlet and reduces net filling.

3. BASIC RATE FORMULAS
3.1 Single inlet pipe
If a pipe fills the tank in N hours:
Rate = 1/N (tank per hour)

3.2 Single outlet pipe
If a pipe empties the tank in M hours:
Rate = -1/M (tank per hour)

3.3 Combined pipes
Add the rates of inlet pipes and subtract the rates of outlet pipes.

4. COMMON FORMULAS
4.1 Two inlet pipes
A fills in x hours, B fills in y hours.
Total rate = 1/x + 1/y
Time required = 1 / (1/x + 1/y) = xy / (x + y)

4.2 One inlet and one outlet
A fills in x hours, B empties in y hours.
Total rate = 1/x - 1/y
If total rate > 0 → tank fills.
If total rate < 0 → tank empties.
Time = 1 / |total rate|

4.3 Two inlets and one outlet
A fills in x hours, B fills in y hours, C empties in z hours.
Total rate = 1/x + 1/y - 1/z
Time = 1 / total rate

4.4 General rule for n pipes
Total rate = Σ(1/inlet hours) - Σ(1/outlet hours)
Time = 1 / total rate

5. PROBLEM-SOLVING STEPS
Step 1: Convert each pipe to its hourly rate.
Step 2: Use positive sign for inlet pipes and negative sign for outlets.
Step 3: Add all rates to get the net rate.
Step 4: If the net rate is positive, the tank will fill; if negative, it will empty.
Step 5: Compute time as 1 / net rate.

6. TIPS AND SHORTCUTS
- Always work with rates, not hours.
- Outlet pipes always contribute negative work.
- Use LCM to add fractions.
- Do not add hours directly.
- If net rate is zero, the tank neither fills nor empties.
- If asked for partial filling, multiply the fraction by the rate.

7. DETAILED EXAMPLES
Example 1: Two inlet pipes
A fills in 12 hours and B fills in 18 hours. Find the time taken together.
Rate of A = 1/12
Rate of B = 1/18
Net rate = 1/12 + 1/18 = 3/36 + 2/36 = 5/36
Time = 36/5 = 7.2 hours

Example 2: Inlet and outlet
A fills in 8 hours, B empties in 24 hours. Find the time to fill.
Rate of A = 1/8
Rate of B = -1/24
Net rate = 1/8 - 1/24 = 3/24 - 1/24 = 2/24 = 1/12
Time = 12 hours

Example 3: Two inlets and an outlet
A fills in 10 hours, B fills in 15 hours, C empties in 30 hours.
Rate of A = 1/10
Rate of B = 1/15
Rate of C = -1/30
Net rate = 1/10 + 1/15 - 1/30 = 3/30 + 2/30 - 1/30 = 4/30 = 2/15
Time = 15/2 = 7.5 hours

Example 4: Leak in the tank
A pipe fills in 20 hours. A leak empties 1/40 tank per hour. Net time?
Rate of A = 1/20
Rate of leak = -1/40
Net rate = 1/20 - 1/40 = 2/40 - 1/40 = 1/40
Time = 40 hours

Example 5: Different working durations
A fills in 6 hours and works for 2 hours alone. Then B (fills in 9 hours) joins. How much more time to fill?
Rate of A = 1/6
Work done by A in 2 hours = 2 × 1/6 = 1/3
Remaining work = 2/3
Rate of B = 1/9
Combined rate = 1/6 + 1/9 = 5/18
Time to finish = (2/3) ÷ (5/18) = 12/5 = 2.4 hours
Total time from start = 2 + 2.4 = 4.4 hours

8. SPECIAL CASES
8.1 When the outlet is faster than the inlet
If |outlet rate| > inlet rate, the tank empties.

8.2 When pipes open at different times
Calculate work done by each pipe for its active duration, then add.

8.3 Partial filling
If asked to fill 3/4 of the tank and net rate is R, time = (3/4) / R.

9. COMMON MISTAKES
- Treating outlet pipes as positive.
- Adding hours directly instead of adding rates.
- Forgetting to subtract leak or outlet rates.
- Miscalculating LCM.
- Assuming time = sum of hours.

11. PRACTICE QUESTIONS
1. Pipe A fills in 10 hours, B fills in 15 hours, C empties in 30 hours. Time together?
Answer: 7.5 hours

2. Pipe A fills in 12 hours, B empties in 20 hours. Can the tank fill? If yes, how long?
Solution:
Net rate = 1/12 - 1/20 = 1/30. Time = 30 hours.

3. A pipe fills a tank in 18 hours. After 6 hours, a second pipe that can fill in 9 hours joins. When is the tank full?
Solution:
Work by first pipe in 6 hours = 6 × 1/18 = 1/3.
Remaining = 2/3.
Combined rate = 1/18 + 1/9 = 1/6.
Time to finish = (2/3) ÷ (1/6) = 4 hours.
Total = 10 hours.

4. A leak empties a tank in 20 hours. An inlet fills it in 10 hours. Time when both are open?
Answer: 20 hours

12. APPLICATIONS
- Home and office water supply systems
- Industrial storage tanks
- Sewage and drainage management
- Chemical mixing vessels
- Irrigation systems
- Oil and fuel tanks

13. FINAL CHECKLIST
- Always convert to rates.
- Use positive for inlets; negative for outlets.
- Add all rates to find the net rate.
- Use reciprocal to find time.
- Simplify using LCM.
- Confirm whether the tank fills or empties.

Pipes and cisterns is a direct application of Time and Work. Practice different combinations and duration changes to build confidence.
