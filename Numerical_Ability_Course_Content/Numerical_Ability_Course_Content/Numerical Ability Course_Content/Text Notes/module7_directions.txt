============================================================
DIRECTIONS & ORIENTATION (DETAILED NOTES)
============================================================

1. OVERVIEW

Direction-sense questions test spatial reasoning: tracking movements, interpreting turns, computing final direction/position, and finding shortest paths on grids. Solve systematically using diagrams, coordinate mapping, or vector addition.

============================================================
2. CARDINAL & INTERCARDINAL DIRECTIONS

- Cardinal: North (N), South (S), East (E), West (W).
- Intercardinal: Northeast (NE), Northwest (NW), Southeast (SE), Southwest (SW) — each lies at 45° between adjacent cardinal directions.

Compass bearings: measured clockwise from North. Example: East = 90°, South = 180°, West = 270°.

============================================================
3. TURNING & ANGLES

- Right turn = rotate 90° clockwise from current facing.
- Left turn = rotate 90° anticlockwise.
- U-turn = rotate 180°.
- Quarter-turns and half-turns combine to give intermediate bearings.

When problems state "turn right" or "turn left", track the current facing direction (not absolute East/West unless given).

============================================================
4. COORDINATE METHOD (RELIABLE FOR POSITION/TOTAL DISPLACEMENT)

Map directions to x-y coordinates:
- North = +y, South = −y, East = +x, West = −x.
- NE = (+d/√2, +d/√2) if distance d at 45°; in grid problems NE often means equal steps North and East.

Procedure:
1) Start at (0,0) facing a given direction (or assume North if none).
2) Convert each move into (Δx, Δy) and add cumulatively.
3) Final position = (ΣΔx, ΣΔy). Distance from start = sqrt((ΣΔx)^2 + (ΣΔy)^2) (use Manhattan distance if asked for grid steps).

Shortcut: when movements are along grid axes, treat steps as integer x/y increments; Pythagorean only for Euclidean distance.

============================================================
5. RELATIVE FACING vs ABSOLUTE DIRECTION

- Absolute statement: "walk 10 m East" means direction fixed to East.
- Relative statement: "turn right and walk 10 m" means turn relative to current facing then move.

Always track the facing orientation variable when turns are given.

============================================================
6. COMMON PROBLEM TYPES & SOLUTION STRATEGIES

a) Final direction after sequence of turns
- Track facing orientation; apply rotations for each turn; final orientation maps to N/E/S/W/NE etc.

b) Final position after moves
- Use coordinate method; add Δx, Δy components.

c) Shortest distance back to start
- If final (x,y) known, Euclidean shortest = sqrt(x^2+y^2); grid (only N/S/E/W allowed) shortest = |x|+|y|.

d) Which turn leads to X direction?
- Back-calculate or simulate; drawing helps for tricky sequences.

e) Clockwise/anticlockwise comparison
- Determine net rotation (sum of signed angles) to find net orientation change.

============================================================
7. WORKED EXAMPLES

Example 1 — Final direction (simple)
Q: Start facing North. Turn right, then right, then left. What is final facing?
- Start N → right → E → right → S → left → E. Final = East.

Example 2 — Final position (grid)
Q: From origin, move 5 km North, 3 km East, 2 km South, 1 km West. Find final coordinates and distance from start.
- Δy = +5 − 2 = +3; Δx = +3 − 1 = +2 ⇒ final (2,3).
- Euclidean distance = sqrt(2^2 + 3^2) = sqrt(13) km.

Example 3 — Relative turns
Q: Facing East. Sequence: walk 10, turn left, walk 5, turn left, walk 10. Final direction?
- Start E; after left → North; after second left → West. Final = West.

Example 4 — Compass bearings
Q: A walks 10 m East, then turns left and walks 10 m. In which direction from start is A now?
- East then left (to North) → final vector (10,10) → bearing NE (45° from North) and distance = sqrt(200).

8. PRACTICE PROBLEMS (WITH ANSWERS)

1) Start at origin facing North. Walk 8 east? (clarify) — wait ensure clear wording.  Rewrite proper problems below.

a) Start at origin, face North. Move 6 km North, turn right, move 4 km, turn right, move 6 km, turn left, move 2 km. What is final direction? Answer: East.

b) From (0,0) walk: N5, E3, S2, W1. Final coordinates? Answer: (2,3). Distance = sqrt(13).

c) Start facing East. Turn left, walk 10, turn right, walk 5. Final facing? Answer: East → left → North → right → East; final = East.

d) A person walks 10m West, 10m South, 10m East, 10m North. Where is he? Answer: Starting point (0 displacement).

e) If you face South and turn 3 times right, what is your final direction? Right turn 3×90° = 270° clockwise from South → equivalent to left 90° → East. Answer: East.

Answers summary: a) East, b) (2,3), sqrt(13), c) East, d) Start, e) East.

============================================================
9. TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

- Use cancelation: consecutive opposite moves cancel (N then S).
- Keep facing as one of four states (N,E,S,W) and update by adding/subtracting 90° for turns.
- Convert complex turn sequences to net rotation: count rights as +90°, lefts as −90° (or vice versa) and reduce mod 360.
- For repeated patterns, compute net per cycle and multiply.

============================================================
10. COMMON MISTAKES & HOW TO AVOID THEM

- Confusing left/right relative to facing — draw or maintain facing variable.
- Mixing up axes: label x (East positive), y (North positive).
- Using Euclidean distance when question expects grid (Manhattan) distance — read question carefully.

============================================================
11. EXTENDED TOPICS

- 3D orientation (up/down) — rarely tested in basic direction-sense.
- Bearing problems using angles not multiple of 90° — use vector components or trig for exact bearings.
- Robot movement puzzles with obstacles — simulate or use BFS for shortest path when complex.
