BASICS OF PERMUTATIONS AND COMBINATIONS - DETAILED NOTES

1. INTRODUCTION
Permutations and combinations are counting techniques used to determine how many ways objects can be arranged or selected. These methods are essential for aptitude, probability, and decision-making problems.

2. WHY THIS TOPIC MATTERS
- Helps solve arrangement and selection problems.
- Appears frequently in competitive exams.
- Underpins probability and statistics.
- Useful in scheduling, coding, team formation, and inventory planning.

3. FUNDAMENTAL COUNTING PRINCIPLE
If one task can happen in m ways and another independent task can happen in n ways, then both tasks together can happen in m × n ways.

Example:
A student has 4 shirts and 3 trousers.
Total combinations = 4 × 3 = 12.

4. FACTORIAL NOTATION
Factorial of n, written n!, means:
n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ... × 2 × 1

Examples:
1! = 1
2! = 2
3! = 6
4! = 24
5! = 120
0! = 1 (important special case)

Factorial simplification:
7!/5! = 7 × 6 = 42
10!/8! = 10 × 9 = 90

5. PERMUTATION: WHEN ORDER MATTERS
Permutations count arrangements where order is important.

5.1 Formula for permutations
The number of ways to choose and arrange r objects from n objects:
P(n, r) = nPr = n! / (n − r)!

5.2 Special case: permutations of n objects in n positions
P(n, n) = n!.

Example:
How many ways can 3 books be arranged on a shelf?
3! = 6.

Example:
From 5 students, select 3 to sit in a row.
Number of ways = P(5, 3) = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60.

5.3 Circular permutations
For arranging n objects in a circle:
Number of ways = (n − 1)!.

6. COMBINATION: WHEN ORDER DOES NOT MATTER
Combinations count selections where order does not matter.

6.1 Formula for combinations
The number of ways to choose r objects from n objects:
C(n, r) = nCr = n! / (r! × (n − r)!)

Example:
Choose 3 friends from 5 to form a team.
Number of ways = C(5, 3) = 10.

6.2 Relationship between permutation and combination
P(n, r) = nCr × r!.

7. DECIDING PERMUTATION VS COMBINATION
- If the problem asks for arrangements, rankings, or order-specific positions → permutation.
- If it asks for groups, teams, or selections without order → combination.

Examples:
- Assigning Gold, Silver, Bronze = permutation.
- Selecting a committee = combination.
- Arranging books = permutation.
- Choosing fruits from a basket = combination.

8. IMPORTANT PROPERTIES
8.1 Symmetry property
nCr = nC(n − r)

8.2 Pascal’s identity
nCr = (n − 1)C(r − 1) + (n − 1)Cr

8.3 Sum of combinations
∑_{r=0}^{n} nCr = 2^n

9. COMMON FORMULAS AND SHORTCUTS
- nPr = n × (n − 1) × ... × (n − r + 1)
- nCr = nPr / r!
- nPr = n! / (n − r)!
- nCr = n! / (r! (n − r)!)

10. SOLVED EXAMPLES
Example 1: Counting arrangements
In how many ways can the letters of the word CAT be arranged?
Number of permutations = 3! = 6.

Example 2: Position-specific selection
From 5 players, how many ways can a captain, vice-captain, and manager be chosen?
This is P(5, 3) = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60.

Example 3: Selecting a team
From 8 students, choose 3 for a committee.
This is C(8, 3) = 8! / (3! × 5!) = 56.

Example 4: Combination with complementary counting
How many ways to choose 4 students out of 10 if one particular student must be included?
Choose remaining 3 from 9: C(9, 3) = 84.

Example 5: Mixed selection and arrangement
Choose 2 captains from 6 players and arrange them in first and second positions.
Step 1: choose 2 players → C(6, 2) = 15.
Step 2: arrange them → 2! = 2.
Total = 15 × 2 = 30.

11. COMMON MISTAKES
- Confusing order-sensitive and order-insensitive problems.
- Using nCr when nPr is needed.
- Forgetting 0! = 1.
- Adding instead of multiplying independent choices.
- Ignoring restrictions such as identical items or fixed positions.

12. PRACTICE QUESTIONS
1. How many 4-digit numbers can be formed using digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 without repetition?
Answer: P(5, 4) = 120.

2. In how many ways can a 3-member committee be formed from 7 people?
Answer: C(7, 3) = 35.

3. How many ways can the letters of the word LEVEL be arranged?
Answer: 5! / (2! × 2!) = 30.

4. A password consists of 3 letters followed by 2 digits. How many passwords are possible if repetition is allowed?
Answer: 26^3 × 10^2 = 175760.

13. APPLICATIONS
- Lottery and raffle probability
- Seating and schedule planning
- Team selection and resource allocation
- Computer science algorithms
- Cryptography and encoding

14. STRATEGIES FOR SUCCESS
- Identify whether order matters first.
- Write nPr or nCr formula clearly.
- Simplify factorial expressions before calculating.
- Use complementary counting when direct counting is difficult.
- Practice with both simple and restricted problems.

15. QUICK SUMMARY
- Counting principle: multiply independent choices.
- Factorial: n! = n × (n − 1) × ... × 1.
- Permutation: order matters → nPr = n!/(n − r)!.
- Combination: order does not matter → nCr = n!/(r! (n − r)!).
- Use nCr for selection, nPr for arrangement.

Permutations and combinations form the foundation of discrete counting. Build comfort with these formulas and apply them carefully to ordered and unordered problems.
