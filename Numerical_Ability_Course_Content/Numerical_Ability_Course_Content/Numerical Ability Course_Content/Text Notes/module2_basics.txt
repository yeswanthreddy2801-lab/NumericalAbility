BASICS OF PERMUTATIONS AND COMBINATIONS

## Introduction

Permutations and Combinations are mathematical techniques used to count the number of possible arrangements and selections without listing every possibility. These concepts are widely used in aptitude tests, probability, computer science, cryptography, scheduling, and data analysis.

Before learning permutations and combinations, it is important to understand counting principles and factorials.

--------------------------------------------------

## Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you will be able to:

• Apply the Fundamental Principle of Counting
• Understand factorial notation
• Simplify factorial expressions
• Distinguish between arrangements and selections
• Decide whether a problem requires permutation or combination

--------------------------------------------------

## Fundamental Principle of Counting

If one task can be performed in m ways and another independent task can be performed in n ways, then both tasks together can be performed in:

Total Ways = m × n

Example:

A student has 4 shirts and 3 trousers.

Number of dressing combinations

= 4 × 3

= 12

--------------------------------------------------

## Factorial

Factorial is represented by !

Definition:

n!

= n × (n−1) × (n−2)...×1

Examples

1! = 1

2! = 2

3! = 6

4! = 24

5! = 120

6! = 720

Important Rule

0! = 1

--------------------------------------------------

## Factorial Simplification

Example 1

7!/5!

= (7×6×5!)/5!

= 42

Example 2

10!/8!

= 10×9

= 90

--------------------------------------------------

## Counting Principle Examples

Example 1

A restaurant offers

5 starters

4 main courses

3 desserts

Total meals

= 5 × 4 × 3

= 60

Example 2

Vehicle numbers consist of

2 letters

followed by

3 digits.

Letters = 26 choices each

Digits = 10 choices each

Total possibilities

= 26 × 26 × 10 × 10 × 10

## Identifying the Question Type

If ORDER matters

→ Permutation

If ORDER does NOT matter

→ Combination

Examples

Selecting a cricket team

→ Combination

Assigning Gold, Silver and Bronze medals

→ Permutation

Arranging books on a shelf

→ Permutation

Selecting committee members

→ Combination

--------------------------------------------------

## Common Mistakes

• Forgetting that 0! = 1

• Confusing selection with arrangement

• Multiplying instead of using factorial

• Using wrong counting principle

## Interview Questions

1. What is factorial?

Answer:
Product of all positive integers from n to 1.

2. What is 0!?

Answer:
1

3. What is the Fundamental Principle of Counting?

Answer:
If one event occurs in m ways and another in n ways, total ways are m × n.

4. Why do we learn factorial before permutation?

Answer:
Permutation and Combination formulas use factorials.

5. Which is more important—the formula or identifying the question type?

Answer:
Identifying the question type.
