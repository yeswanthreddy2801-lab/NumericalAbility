PROBABILITY - COINS & DICE (DETAILED NOTES)

1. INTRODUCTION
Probability measures how likely an event is to happen. This topic is essential for aptitude tests, games, statistics, and decision-making.

2. BASIC DEFINITIONS
- Experiment: An action that produces outcomes (e.g., toss a coin, roll a die).
- Outcome: A possible result of the experiment.
- Sample space (S): Set of all possible outcomes.
- Event: A subset of the sample space.
- Probability of event A: P(A) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of outcomes.

3. PROBABILITY RULES
- 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1
- P(∅) = 0 (impossible event)
- P(S) = 1 (certain event)
- Complement rule: P(A') = 1 − P(A)
- Additive rule for mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
- Independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

4. COIN PROBABILITY
4.1 One coin
Sample space: S = {H, T}
Total outcomes = 2
P(H) = 1/2, P(T) = 1/2

4.2 Two coins
Sample space: S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}
Total outcomes = 4
Examples:
- P(exactly one head) = 2/4 = 1/2
- P(at least one head) = 3/4
- P(two tails) = 1/4

4.3 n coins
Total outcomes = 2^n
Example:
- 3 coins → 8 outcomes
- 4 coins → 16 outcomes

5. DICE PROBABILITY
5.1 One die
Sample space: S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}
Total outcomes = 6
Examples:
- P(rolling a 5) = 1/6
- P(rolling an even number) = 3/6 = 1/2
- P(rolling a number ≥ 4) = 3/6 = 1/2

5.2 Two dice
Total outcomes = 6 × 6 = 36
Ordered sample space: all pairs (a, b) with a and b from 1 to 6.

5.3 Sum of two dice
Number of ways to get each sum:
- Sum 2: 1 way
- Sum 3: 2 ways
- Sum 4: 3 ways
- Sum 5: 4 ways
- Sum 6: 5 ways
- Sum 7: 6 ways
- Sum 8: 5 ways
- Sum 9: 4 ways
- Sum 10: 3 ways
- Sum 11: 2 ways
- Sum 12: 1 way

Probability of each sum = favorable / 36.
Example: P(sum = 7) = 6/36 = 1/6.
Example: P(sum = 9) = 4/36 = 1/9.

5.4 More than one die
For n dice, total outcomes = 6^n.
Example: 3 dice → 216 outcomes.

6. EVENT TYPES
6.1 Simple event
Event with a single outcome.
Example: rolling a 5 on one die.

6.2 Compound event
Event with multiple outcomes.
Example: getting an even number on one die.

6.3 Complement event
The event that A does not occur.
Example: P(no head with two coins) = 1 − P(at least one head).

6.4 At least one event
Use complement to solve faster.
Example: P(at least one head in three coins) = 1 − P(no head) = 1 − 1/8 = 7/8.

7. COIN AND DICE SAMPLE SPACE EXAMPLES
7.1 Three coins
Total outcomes = 8. S = {HHH, HHT, HTH, THH, HTT, THT, TTH, TTT}.

7.2 Two dice one even, one odd
Favorable outcomes = 18. P = 18/36 = 1/2.

8. SOLVED EXAMPLES
Example 1: One coin
P(head) = 1/2.

Example 2: Two coins
P(exactly one head) = 2/4 = 1/2.

Example 3: One die
P(rolling a multiple of 3) = 2/6 = 1/3.

Example 4: Two dice
P(sum equals 8) = 5/36.

Example 5: Coin and die combined
Toss one coin and roll one die. Total outcomes = 2 × 6 = 12.
Example: P(head and even number) = (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/4.

9. COMMON MISTAKES
- Ignoring whether outcomes are ordered or unordered.
- Using 6^n for coins or 2^n for dice.
- Forgetting replacement vs no replacement.
- Applying addition when events are not mutually exclusive.
- Failing to use the complement for “at least” questions.

10. SHORTCUTS AND TIPS
- Use 2^n for n coin tosses.
- Use 6^n for n dice rolls.
- List the sample space for small problems.
- Use complementary probability for at least one cases.
- Simplify fractions early.

11. PRACTICE QUESTIONS
1. What is the probability of getting no heads in 3 coin tosses?
Answer: 1/8.

2. What is the probability of getting a sum of 5 with two dice?
Answer: 4/36 = 1/9.

3. What is the probability of rolling at least one 6 in two dice?
Answer: 11/36.

4. What is the probability of getting exactly two heads in 3 coin tosses?
Answer: 3/8.

12. REAL-WORLD APPLICATIONS
- Board games and gambling.
- Weather forecasting.
- Risk assessment.
- Quality control.
- Statistical predictions.

13. QUICK SUMMARY
- Probability = favorable / total.
- Coin tosses produce 2^n outcomes.
- Dice rolls produce 6^n outcomes.
- Use complement for “at least” and “none” cases.
- Count favorable outcomes carefully.

Coins and dice problems build your understanding of basic probability. Practice these concepts to gain speed and accuracy.
