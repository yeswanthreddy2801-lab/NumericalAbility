NUMBER SERIES — DETAILED NOTES

============================================================
1. OVERVIEW

Number-series questions present a sequence of numbers that follow a rule. Your goal is to identify the rule, predict the next term(s), or find the missing term. Commonly tested in aptitude and placement exams, these problems require quick pattern recognition and systematic checking.

============================================================
2. QUICK SOLVING STRATEGY

1) Convert the sequence into numeric list and compute first differences (Δ1).
2) If Δ1 is not constant, compute second differences (Δ2), third differences, etc. Constant k-th difference suggests a polynomial of degree k.
3) Test multiplicative patterns by dividing successive terms (ratios).
4) Check for alternating/interleaved subsequences: separate odd and even indexed terms and analyze each.
5) Consider digit-based or positional rules (sum of digits, product of digits, reversing digits).
6) If many rules fit, prefer the simplest (Occam's razor) and check examples provided.

============================================================
3. COMMON PATTERN TYPES

A. Arithmetic progression (AP)
- Rule: add/subtract constant d. Example: 5, 8, 11, 14 (d=3).

B. Geometric progression (GP)
- Rule: multiply/divide by constant r. Example: 3, 6, 12, 24 (r=2).

C. Polynomial sequences (squares, cubes, triangular numbers)
- n^2: 1,4,9,16; n^3:1,8,27,64; triangular: 1,3,6,10,15.
- Detect by constant second differences (for quadratic) or constant third differences (for cubic).

D. Recursive / cumulative rules
- Each term depends on previous terms (e.g., Fibonacci: 1,1,2,3,5,8 where t_n = t_{n-1}+t_{n-2}).

E. Alternating / Interleaved patterns
- Sequence built from two or more independent subsequences. Example: 2,5,4,8,6,11 → odd terms 2,4,6 (+2) and even terms 5,8,11 (+3).

F. Multiplicative + additive mixes
- t_{n+1} = a × t_n + b. Example: 2,5,12,29 → ×2 +1, ×2 +2, ×2 +5 (pattern may change).

G. Digit-based rules
- Based on digits: sum of digits, reverse digits, product of digits, or apply operation to digits to get next term.

H. Prime, factorial, and special-index sequences
- Primes: 2,3,5,7,11. Factorials: 1,2,6,24,120. Indexed sequences: terms at prime indices.

============================================================
4. DIAGNOSTIC CHECKLIST (WHAT TO TRY FIRST)

- Compute differences (Δ1, Δ2).
- Compute ratios between successive terms.
- Split into odd/even index subsequences.
- Check small-degree polynomials via constant Δ2 or Δ3.
- Check for known sequences: Fibonacci, primes, squares, cubes, factorials, triangular numbers.
- Look for digit operations or concatenations.

============================================================
5. WORKED EXAMPLES

Example 1 — AP
Sequence: 7, 12, 17, 22, ?
- Δ = +5 ⇒ next = 22 + 5 = 27.

Example 2 — GP
Sequence: 3, 9, 27, 81, ?
- Ratio = ×3 ⇒ next = 81×3 = 243.

Example 3 — Quadratic (n^2)
Sequence: 1,4,9,16,25 ⇒ squares ⇒ next = 36.

Example 4 — Fibonacci
Sequence: 1,1,2,3,5,8,? ⇒ next = 13.

Example 5 — Mixed cumulative
Sequence: 2, 3, 5, 9, 17, ?
- Differences: 1,2,4,8 → powers of 2. Next difference = 16 ⇒ next term = 17+16 = 33.

Example 6 — Alternating subsequences
Sequence: 2, 5, 4, 8, 6, 11, ?
- Odd-index terms: 2,4,6 → +2 each. Even-index terms: 5,8,11 → +3 each. Next odd-term = 8, next even-term = 14 ⇒ next term = 8 (since sequence continues odd/even ordering) — check indexing carefully.

Example 7 — Polynomial detection
Sequence: 2, 6, 12, 20, 30
- Δ1: 4,6,8,10 (not constant). Δ2: 2,2,2 (constant) ⇒ quadratic; next Δ1 adds 12 ⇒ next term = 30+12 = 42.

============================================================
6. DIGIT-BASED EXAMPLES

1) Reverse digits: 12, 21, 13, 31... alternates between number and its reverse.
2) Sum-digit sequences: start with 19 → next = 1+9=10 → next = 1+0=1 (can be used to generate checks).

============================================================
8. TIPS FOR TESTS

- Start with simple differences and ratios.
- When stuck, try separating into subsequences.
- For polynomial-like sequences compute higher-order differences.
- Use scratch to track multiple hypotheses quickly; pick the rule that fits all given terms.

============================================================
9. COMMON MISTAKES

- Overfitting a complex rule when a simple rule exists.
- Neglecting wrap-around behavior for digit operations.
- Assuming multiplicative when additive fits.
